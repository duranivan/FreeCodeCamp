Johann Carl Friedrich Gauss

The Prince of Mathematics

Carl Friedrich Gauß (1777–1855).

Here's a time line of Johann Carl Friedrich Gauss: (for a brief graphical timeline, click here.)

  • 1777 - Born in Braunschweig, Germany.
  • 1791 - Starts receiving financial support from the Duke of Brunswick.
  • 1795 - Joins the University of Göttingen.
  • 1801 - Publishes 'Disquisitiones Arithmeticae', the foundamental work of algebraic number theory, at the age of 21.
  • 1805 - Marries Johanna Osthoff.
  • 1807 - Accepts position at Göttingen, where he would be in charge of the new observatory, with no teaching duties.
  • 1809 - Marries Minna Waldeck, after his first wife's death in the same year.
  • 1812 - Starts his work on elliptic integrals, the arithmetic-geometric mean, and hypergeometric functions.
  • 1818 - Starts directing personally the geodetic survey of the Kingdom of Hanover. This leads to his publication 'Determination of the latitude difference between Göttingen and Altona' (1828), which includes a development of the theory of least-squares fitting of data (linear regression).
  • 1823 - Wins the Copenhagen Prize Essay- general conformality criterion for mapping between two arbitrary surfaces, using the Cauchy-Riemann equations.
  • 1828 - Publishes 'General investigations on curved surfaces', Gauss’s main work on differential geometry. Introduces the `Gaussian curvature’ and the program of investigating intrinsic differential geometry.
  • 1831 - Wilhelm Weber joins the Göttingen faculty as professor of physics; this marks the beginning of Gauss’s systematic research in physics, especially potential theory.
  • 1832 - Starts his research on terrestrial magnetism, encouraged by Weber and Alexander v. Humboldt.
  • 1838 - Develops, together with Weber, the first functioning electromagnetic telegraph.
  • 1855 - Dies in Göttingen, Germany.

"The best that Gauss has given us was likewise an exclusive production. If he had not created his geometry of surfaces, which served Riemann as a basis, it is scarcely conceivable that anyone else would have discovered it. I do not hesitate to confess that to a certain extent a similar pleasure may be found by absorbing ourselves in questions of pure geometry."

Albert Einstein
